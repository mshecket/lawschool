Criminal Law Exam Practice

 

People v. Eulo (criminal homicide)

 

  1. Voluntary act – Under the Model Penal Code § 2.01, a person is not guilty of an offense unless his liability is based on conduct which includes a voluntary act. In this case, Eulo’s willed bodily movement in pulling the trigger of the gun with which he shot his victim constitutes the required voluntary act.
  2. Social harm – The social harm in this case is the death of the victim, but it is in dispute what constitutes death in this case. The Model Penal Code is silent on the definition of “death”.  The defendant, citing common law precedent, claims that the social harm envisioned by the Model Penal Code is the cessation of circulatory and respiratory activity.  The People, on the other hand, argue that “brain death” is, or should be, the social harm.
  3. Mens rea – In order to be convicted of criminal homicide under the Model Penal Code § 210.1(1), the defendant must have caused the victim’s death purposely, knowingly, recklessly, or negligently.  There is no evidence in the record on this appeal of the state of mind of the defendant at the time of the killing. However, because the defendant does not raise this issue on appeal and the record describes the defendant as having “shot his victim in the head”, it can be inferred that the mens rea of purpose was proven at trial such that it is not at issue now. The Model Penal Code § 210.2(1)(a) would classify the defendant’s offense as murder, which is a felony of the first degree (§ 210.2(2)).
  4. Actual causation – Under the Model Penal Code § 2.03(1)(a), the defendant’s conduct is the cause of the resulting offense when “it is an antecedent but for which the result in question would not have occurred”. To determine whether the defendant is an actual cause of the social harm in this case, we ask, more specifically: “But for the voluntary act of the defendant, would the social harm have occurred when it did?”  If the defendant had not shot the victim, the victim would not have become brain dead when he did, and, alternatively, would not have ceased respiration and circulation when he did. Therefore, the defendant is an actual cause of the death of the victim.

    There are no additional causal requirements in the Code as contemplated in § 2.03(1)(b).
  5. Proximate causation – The Model Penal Code disposes of the proximate causation issue in short order. Because purposely causing a certain result (the death of the victim) was an element of the defendant’s offense, and because the actual result involves, arguably, the same kind of injury or harm as that contemplated by the defendant, we consult § 2.03(2)(b).  This section tells us that because the actual result (death under the traditional definition) is not too remote or accidental from the contemplated result that the defendant alleges (brain death) to have an effect on the defendant’s liability.

 

 

Outline EP-8

 

Bob returned home one day and, to his shock, observed his wife in bed with Victor. Enraged, Bob stalked out of the house, walked next door, and angrily asked Ann, his neighbor, for a gun that he knew that she lawfully kept for self-protection. When she asked him why he needed it, he angrily stated, “I need a gun to kill someone.” Ann gave him her gun, saying, “Go get him.”

 

Bob returned to his house and walked into the bedroom, gun pointed. When he entered, Victor, by now out of the bed, had a large kitchen knife in his hand. From a distance of 10 feet, Bob shot Victor four times, killing him instantly. Because of his rage, Bob had not seen the knife in Victor’s hand when he shot him.

 

Discuss Bob’s and Ann’s criminal liability, if any, for the death of Victor. Apply [the Model Penal Code.]

 

State v. Bob (Homicide)

 

  1. Voluntary act – The Model Penal Code requires Bob’s conduct to include a voluntary act in order for him to be liable for any offense, § 2.01(1).  Bob’s conduct included the voluntary act of shooting Victor.
  2. Social harm – The social harm is the killing of a human being (Victor) by another human being (Bob).
  3. Mens rea – Under the Model Penal Code, in order to be guilty of criminal homicide, a person must commit the actus reus of killing another human being purposely, knowingly, recklessly, or negligently, § 210.1.  There is evidence that Bob acted with purpose in killing Victor. Bob told Ann that he was planning to kill someone.  [Bob also had the motive to kill Victor: he was angry at him for sleeping with his wife.] This would make Bob guilty of murder, § 210.2(1)(a), unless a jury can be persuaded that Bob was acting under the influence of extreme mental or emotional disturbance for which there is a reasonable explanation or excuse, § 210.3(1)(b), in which case Bob’s crime would be manslaughter.

    Bob could argue that witnessing his wife in an apparent act of adultery enraged him so much that he experienced extreme emotional distress that continued through the time of the actual killing.  This would be a threshold question of law for the judge. If the judge allowed this argument to go before a jury, Bob would further need to persuade the jury that the disturbance was reasonable.  In doing this, Bob needs to try to get the jury to sympathize with his position and imagine being in his shoes.

    The State would argue, however, that Bob had adequate time in the course of going to his neighbor’s house and back that he either no longer was under the influence of extreme distress or that such distress was no longer reasonable at the time of the killing. It could be argued that, in essence, no one could stay that mad for that long (a question for the judge), or that, alternatively, no reasonable person could stay that mad for that long (a question for the jury).

    If the fact is conceded that Bob was so enraged that he did not see the knife in Victor’s hand, Bob could argue that this is evidence that his extreme emotional disturbance continued to the moment of the killing.  Bob could claim that no one could fail to perceive the danger of another person holding a knife unless they were experiencing extreme distress of the nature contemplated by the Model Penal Code.

    [The purpose of allowing the partial defense of extreme emotional or mental disturbance is to recognize that society sees an actor as less at fault if they are not in full control of their actions due to a disturbance with an understandable or reasonable cause.  In other words, a retributivist would find that Bob did not act fully by choice, and thus his culpability is limited by his ability to freely choose not to commit the offense.  From a utilitarian perspective, on the other hand, someone in a rage like Bob may not be deterred from committing murder, and therefore it serves no purpose to punish him for murder because, arguably, no net social gain will result.]
  4. Actual causation – Under the Model Penal Code, Bob’s conduct was an actual cause of the death of Victor when Victor’s death would not have occurred but for Bob’s voluntary act. If Bob had not shot Victor, Victor would not have died.
  5. Proximate causation – There are no intervening causes between the firing of the gun and the death of Victor.
  6. Defenses – Bob’s killing of Victor was justified for self-protection if Bob believed that such force was immediately necessary to protect himself against the use of unlawful force by Victor, § 3.04(1).  Because Bob used deadly force, Bob is not justified unless he believed that deadly force was necessary to protect himself from being killed or seriously injured by Victor, § 3.04(2)(b). Bob’s case for justification by self-protection is weak for several reasons: first, he did not see the knife in Victor’s hand, which makes the use of deadly force unjustifiable given the circumstances as Bob believed them to be. Furthermore, because Victor was 10 feet away, Bob will have a hard time proving that he believed that force was immediately necessary.

 

State v. Bob (criminal conspiracy)

 

  1. Voluntary act – Bob’s conduct included the voluntary act of agreeing with Ann that Bob would carry out the offense of criminal homicide, §§ 2.01 and 5.03(1)(a).
  2. Social harm – The social harm is the killing of Victor.
  3. Mens rea – Bob acted with the mens rea of purpose in agreeing with Ann to facilitate the commission of criminal homicide.

 

State v. Ann (criminal homicide)

 

  1. Voluntary act – Ann’s conduct included the voluntary acts of giving her gun to Bob and telling him to “go get him”, § 2.01(1).  These acts constituted aid in the commission of Bob’s offense, § 2.06(3)(a)(ii).
  2. Social harm – The social harm in this case is the killing of Victor by Bob.
  3. Mens Rea – Ann may be liable as an accomplice to murder if (1) she had the purpose of promoting or facilitating the offense of murder, § 2.06(3)(a), and (2) she had the mens rea of purpose in regard to result of Bob’s conduct, namely, the killing of Victor, § 2.06(4).

    The State may argue that because Bob told Ann that he was planning to kill someone, Ann’s statement to Bob is evidence that she had both the purpose of facilitating Bob’s offense and the purpose that Bob kill another human being.

 

State v. Ann (criminal conspiracy)

 

  1. Voluntary act – Ann’s conduct included the voluntary act of agreeing with Bob that he would carry out the offense of criminal homicide, §§ 2.01 and § 5.03(1).
  2. Social harm – The social harm was the killing of Victor by Bob.
  3. Mens rea – Ann acted with the mens rea of purpose in agreeing to aid Bob in the commission of his offense, § 5.03(1)(b).
